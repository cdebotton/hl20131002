Sushi

Origin

Sushi, though originally believed to develop in Southeast Asia along the Mekong River, originated from the Edo region during the Edo period (江戸時代) in Japan — 1603-1868.

Tokyo is modern day Edo, the name was changed when Tokyo became the nation's capital.

Sushi, in its simplest form, is vinegared rice and raw fish.

Oh shit, there's more

Sushi is a pretty broad category, here are a few kinds:

Inarizushi

Fried sweet tofu filled with vinegared rice.

Chirashizushi

A bowl of rice topped with sashimi (raw fish).

Makizushi

Sushi that is prepared in a roll of sushi and seaweed.

Nigirizushi

A piece of raw fish placed on top of a small mound of rice.

Kaiseki

What the hell is it?

Kaiseiki is more or less the Japanese equivalent of French Haute cuisine. What's Haute cuisine? Really expensive, really upscale, really pretty food.

Kaiseki can be thought of as banquet food. It typically is served in anywhere between three and twelve courses (or more). It typically begins with miso soup, has vegetable, fish, and red meat courses, and ends with a dessert.

Order

Japanese Kaiseiki usually follows a specific order (not gonna lie, copied and pasted this shit right from Wikipedia, sue me).

Sakizuke (先附)

an appetizer similar to the French amuse-bouche.

Hassun (八寸)

the second course, which sets the seasonal theme. Typically one kind of sushi and several smaller side dishes.

Mukōzuke (向付)

a sliced dish of seasonal sashimi.

Takiawase (煮合)

vegetables served with meat, fish or tofu; the ingredients are simmered separately.

Futamono (蓋物)

a "lidded dish"; typically a soup.

Yakimono (焼物)

(1) flame-broiled food (esp. fish); (2) earthenware, pottery, china.

Su-zakana (酢肴)

a small dish used to clean the palate, such as vegetables in vinegar; vinegared appetizer.

Hiyashi-bachi (冷し鉢)

served only in summer; chilled, lightly cooked vegetables.

Naka-choko (中猪口)

another palate-cleanser; may be a light, acidic soup.

Shiizakana (強肴)

a substantial dish, such as a hot pot.

Gohan (御飯)

a rice dish made with seasonal ingredients.

Kō no mono (香の物)

seasonal pickled vegetables.

Tome-wan (止椀)

a miso-based or vegetable soup served with rice.

Mizumono (水物)

a seasonal dessert; may be fruit, confection, ice cream, or cake.

Okonomiyaki

お好み焼き literally transaltes to "What you love, grilled."

What is it?

Okonomiyaki is a savory pancake typically comprised of cabbage, flour, yam, onions, and various proteins such as pork belly, bacon, squid, shrimp, octopus... It's pretty much whatever the hell you want in it (or whatever the person that's making it wants).

Major Variations

Osaka style is thicker style pancake typically topped with mayo and okonomiyaki sauce. This is predominantly what you'll find in the United States.

Okinawa style is thinner than the Osaka variant, and is typically more of a home cooked item as opposed to something you'd find on the street (at night, probably a little buzzed).

Let's start with Ramen

Although ramen (ラーメン) is often recognized as a Japanese dish, it's actually made with a Chinese style noodle. It is served in a broth of either fish, pork, or chicken stock, and can have any sort of toppings on it. Typically, you will find a sheet of nori, pork belly, a soy-soaked egg, ginger, green onions, enoki mushrooms, and menma (fermented bamboo shoots).

After World War 2, cheap instant noodles were introduced to Japan by Momofuku Ando. By the 1980s, ramen was extremely popular in Japanese culture.

Oh yeah, Soba

Soba is a buckwheat noodle, and is served either chilled or in a hot broth in Japanese cuisine. When served hot, it is often presented in a bowl of a soy based broth with various vegetable and meat toppings. Soba is great if you want noodles but want something healthy, as a typical serving (100g) has about 344 calories.

Cold soba is served with a cold dipping sauce and the toppings are served on the side to mix in with your dipping broth. Often, the hot water that was used to cook the soba is brought out toward the end of the meal to drink.
